## Books

### Foundational Works

- **Baring-Gould, Sabine** (1865). *The Book of Were-Wolves*. Smith, Elder & Co. [Public domain]
  - First comprehensive English-language study of werewolf folklore
  - Compilation of historical cases and folklore from across Europe
  - Influential but reflects Victorian attitudes

- **Summers, Montague** (1933). *The Werewolf*. Kegan Paul.
  - Comprehensive compilation of historical accounts
  - Occultist perspective but extensive research
  - Primary source for many historical cases

### Academic & Historical Analysis

- **Woodward, Ian** (1979). *The Werewolf Delusion*. Paddington Press. ISBN: 978-0448226705
  - Historical analysis of werewolf beliefs
  - Examines trial records and social context
  - Skeptical approach to supernatural claims

- **Otten, Charlotte F.** (1986). *A Lycanthropy Reader: Werewolves in Western Culture*. Syracuse University Press. ISBN: 978-0815623816
  - Academic collection of historical texts
  - Literary and folkloric sources
  - Excellent scholarly resource

- **Douglas, Adam** (1992). *The Beast Within: A History of the Werewolf*. Chapmans. ISBN: 978-1855920545
  - Comprehensive history from ancient to modern
  - Cultural and psychological analysis
  - Well-researched popular history

- **Sconduto, Leslie A.** (2008). *Metamorphoses of the Werewolf: A Literary Study from Antiquity Through the Renaissance*. McFarland. ISBN: 978-0786433735
  - Literary analysis of werewolf evolution
  - Ancient through Renaissance texts
  - Academic scholarly work

### Beast of Gévaudan Specific

- **Smith, Jay M.** (2011). *Monsters of the Gévaudan: The Making of a Beast*. Harvard University Press. ISBN: 978-0674047426
  - Definitive modern historical analysis
  - Examines social and cultural context
  - Most authoritative Beast of Gévaudan study

- **Moriceau, Jean-Marc** (2016). *Histoire du méchant loup: La question des attaques sur l'homme en France (XVe-XXe siècle)*. Fayard. [French]
  - Comprehensive study of wolf attacks in France
  - Historical documentation and analysis
  - Includes Gévaudan in broader context

### Folklore & Mythology

- **Ginzburg, Carlo** (1983/1991). *Ecstasies: Deciphering the Witches' Sabbath*. University of Chicago Press. ISBN: 978-0226296395
  - Examines werewolf beliefs in context of witch trials
  - Shamanistic interpretations
  - Academic folklore study

- **Levi-Strauss, Claude** (1995). *The Story of Lynx*. University of Chicago Press. ISBN: 978-0226474441
  - Anthropological analysis of transformation myths
  - Comparative mythology
  - Includes shapeshifter traditions worldwide

## Academic Papers & Articles

### Medical & Psychiatric

- **Keck, P.E., et al.** (1988). "Lycanthropy: Alive and Well in the Twentieth Century." *Psychological Medicine*, 18(1), 113-120.
  - Clinical lycanthropy case studies
  - Modern psychiatric perspective
  - Documents rare delusional disorder

- **Garlipp, P., et al.** (2004). "Lycanthropy—Psychopathological and Psychodynamical Aspects." *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica*, 109(1), 19-22.
  - Clinical lycanthropy analysis
  - Psychodynamic interpretation
  - Case study documentation

- **Blom, Jan Dirk** (2014). "When Doctors Cry Wolf: A Systematic Review of the Literature on Clinical Lycanthropy." *History of Psychiatry*, 25(1), 87-102.
  - Comprehensive literature review
  - Historical and modern cases
  - Diagnostic considerations

- **Verdoux, H. & Bourgeois, M.** (1993). "A Case of Lycanthropy with Temporal Lobe Epilepsy." *Neuropsychiatry, Neuropsychology, and Behavioral Neurology*, 6(2), 113-114.
  - Links to neurological conditions
  - Case study documentation

### Historical & Folklore Studies

- **Henningsen, Gustav** (1980). "The Papers of Alonso de Salazar Frías: A Spanish Contribution to the History of Witchcraft." *Temenos*, 16, 85-106.
  - Spanish Inquisition werewolf cases
  - Primary source analysis
  - Skepticism in historical context

- **Oates, Caroline** (1989). "Metamorphosis and Lycanthropy in Franche-Comté, 1521-1643." *Fragments for a History of the Human Body*, Part 1, 304-363.
  - Regional werewolf trial analysis
  - Social and cultural context
  - French witch-hunt era

### Anthropological

- **Doniger, Wendy** (1998). "Transformations: From Animals to Humans and Humans to Animals." In *The Implied Spider: Politics and Theology in Myth*. Columbia University Press.
  - Cross-cultural shapeshifter analysis
  - Comparative mythology
  - Transformation symbolism

## Classical & Medieval Texts

### Ancient Sources

- **Petronius** (~1st century CE). *Satyricon* (Section 62)
  - Roman literary werewolf account
  - Early transformation narrative
  - Influential classical source

- **Ovid** (8 CE). *Metamorphoses* (Book I, lines 209-239)
  - Lycaon transformed by Jupiter
  - Foundation myth for lycanthropy term
  - Classical literature cornerstone

- **Pliny the Elder** (77-79 CE). *Natural History* (Book VIII)
  - Ancient Roman werewolf beliefs
  - Skeptical but documents folklore
  - Historical reference

- **Herodotus** (~440 BCE). *Histories* (Book IV)
  - Greek account of Neuri transformation
  - Early ethnographic reference
  - Ancient historical source

### Medieval Texts

- **Gerald of Wales** (1188). *Topographia Hibernica*
  - Irish werewolf accounts
  - Medieval Christian perspective
  - Historical documentation

- **Marie de France** (late 12th century). *Bisclavret*
  - Medieval French werewolf lai
  - Literary werewolf tale
  - Chivalric romance tradition

- **Guillaume de Palerne** (13th century). *Romance of William and the Werewolf*
  - Medieval romance with werewolf
  - Literary tradition development
  - Sympathetic werewolf portrayal

## Medical & Scientific Sources

### Hypertrichosis (Werewolf Syndrome)

- **Macias-Flores, M.A., et al.** (1984). "A New Form of Hypertrichosis Inherited as an X-linked Dominant Trait." *Human Genetics*, 66(1), 66-70.
  - Genetic basis of excessive hair growth
  - Medical condition documentation
  - Possible inspiration for legends

- **Baumeister, F.A., et al.** (1993). "Hypertrichosis Lanuginosa Congenita." *Journal of Medical Genetics*, 30(11), 958-962.
  - Rare genetic disorder study
  - Clinical documentation
  - Historical "wolf-man" cases

### Rabies Connection

- **Gomez-Alonso, Juan** (1998). "Rabies: A Possible Explanation for the Vampire Legend." *Neurology*, 51(3), 856-859.
  - Rabies symptoms and vampire/werewolf legends
  - Medical-historical analysis
  - Symptom correlation

- **Warrell, M.J. & Warrell, D.A.** (2004). "Rabies and a Bite from a Bat." *BMJ*, 329(7479), 1452-1453.
  - Rabies transmission and behavior changes
  - Medical evidence for legend origins

### Porphyria (Disputed Theory)

- **Dolphin, David** (1985). Presentation at American Association for the Advancement of Science.
  - Porphyria as werewolf/vampire explanation
  - Controversial and largely rejected theory
  - Historical interest only

- **Pierach, C.A., et al.** (1987). "Did Vampires Suffer from Porphyria?" *Proceedings (Baylor University Medical Center)*, 1(3), 18-20.
  - Critique of porphyria-vampire/werewolf theory
  - Medical analysis
  - Theory debunking
